The frame

Read every form as an arrow

Two questions answer everything: Who is は (the subject)? and which way does the action point?

する
Plain
A does the action.
The subject is the doer. Action points outward.
される
Passive · 受身
A receives the action.
Subject is on the receiving end. Arrow points back at them.
させる
Causative · 使役
A makes B do the action.
Subject is the instigator. Arrow pushes onto someone else.
させられる
Caus-passive · 使役受身
A is made to do the action.
Subject is the reluctant doer — pushed by someone else.
The shortcut you'll use forever: られる = "receive the action." せる = "cause the action." Stack them — せる + られる = "receive the causing" = be made to do it.

Passive · the part English doesn't have

The "suffering" passive

Japanese can put something in the passive just to say it happened, and it affected me — usually as a nuisance. There's no clean English equivalent, which is why it feels strange.

私わたしは雨あめに降ふられた。
lit. "I was rained-on."
It rained on me (and I got soaked / it ruined my plans).
赤あかちゃんに泣なかれて、眠ねむれなかった。
The baby cried on me, and I couldn't sleep.
Possessive twist: when your thing is acted on — 弟おとうとにケーキを食たべられた = "my cake got eaten by my brother." You're the subject because you're the one wronged, even though the cake is what was eaten.

Causative · the particle trap

を or に for the causee?

The single rule that decides it: is the main verb transitive or intransitive?

Intransitive causee takes を

No object competing for を, so the person takes it.

先生せんせいは学生がくせいを立たたせた。
The teacher made the students stand.
Transitive causee takes に

を is already taken by the object → person gets に.

母ははは子こどもに本ほんを読よませた。
Mom made the child read a book.
Why it matters: you can't have two を in one clause. 子どもを本を読ませる ✗ is ungrammatical. The transitive verb already claimed を for its object, so the causee is pushed to に.
Causative-passive · 使役受身

"I was made to do it."

Both at once: someone forces the action (causative), and you're the one stuck receiving that force (passive). Almost always carries a reluctant / unwilling feeling.

私
subject
(reluctant doer)
は
飲まされる
forced onto me
部長ぶちょう
instigator
(forces)
に
私わたしは部長ぶちょうにお酒さけを飲のまされた。
I was made to drink (alcohol) by my boss.
Notice the flow flipped back. Causative pushed outward; causative-passive points the force at the subject again — because passive always means "the subject receives it."
Causative-passive · formation

Build it in two moves

You already know both halves. This is just passive applied to the causative.

make causative→〜せるthen passive→〜せ + られる

せる conjugates like a る-verb, so making it passive is just drop る + られる. Same move you did on slide 4.

Causative-passive · in the wild

How it sounds in real life

The instigator takes に. The reluctant tone is the whole point.

子こどもの頃ころ、毎日まいにちピアノを練習れんしゅうさせられた。
As a kid, I was made to practice piano every day. (I didn't want to.)
会議かいぎで長ながい時間じかん待またされた。
I was made to wait a long time at the meeting.
先輩せんぱいに嫌きらいな食たべ物ものを食たべさせられた。
My senior made me eat food I hate.

Before you go

Two traps & your recap

Trap 1 Passive = potential (G2)

食べられる is both "is eaten" (passive) and "can eat" (potential). They're identical. Only context tells them apart.

Trap 2 来られる = 3 things

来られる can be passive, potential, or honorific (respectful "come"). Lean on context every time.

The whole thing in four lines
〜れる/られるsubject receives the action · doer takes に
〜せる/させるsubject makes/lets · を for intransitive, に for transitive
〜されるsubject is made to (reluctantly) · instigator takes に
