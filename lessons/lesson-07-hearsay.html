Relay information and identify its source

1. ～ということだ・～とのことだ

Core meaning: Report information you learned from another person, notice, email, or other source.

Formation

普通形 + ということだ／とのことだ（命令・依頼も引用形のまま）

Nuance and use

～ということだ is neutral and explanatory. ～とのことだ is more concise and formal, so it appears often in messages, notices, and business communication. Add ～によると, ～によれば, or ～では when the source matters.

Compare

～そうだ also reports information but focuses less on the exact content or source. ～とのことだ sounds more formal than conversational ～って.

Common mistake

Do not use the polite form inside the report merely because the whole sentence is polite. Report the original content in plain form, then add ということです or とのことです.

Examples

Book example

市のお知らせによれば、このはからが始まるということです。

According to the city notice, construction on this road will begin next week.

Why this works

市のお知らせによれば names the official source; ということです relays its information without quoting the notice directly.

Book example

店の人の話では、このの米はとてもおいしいということだ。

According to someone at the shop, the rice from this region is very delicious.

Why this works

店の人の話では frames the report's source, and ということだ passes on the shop worker's statement.

Book example

さっき川さんからがありました。今日は社に戻れないとのことです。

Mr. Kawamura called a little while ago. He says that he cannot return to the office today.

Why this works

とのことです gives the content of the earlier telephone message in a concise, businesslike style.

Book example

メールによると、林さんははとてもとのことです。

According to the email, Ms. Hayashi will be very busy next week.

Why this works

メールによると supplies a written source, while とのことです formally reports its content.

Book example

【手紙】新しいが決まったとのこと、おめでとうございます。

[Letter] Congratulations on getting a new job.

Why this works

In a letter, とのこと can nominalize the news being acknowledged before the congratulatory message.

によると、はになるということです。

According to the weather forecast, it will snow this weekend.

Why this works

The forecast is explicitly named as the source, and ということです relays its conclusion.

会社のでは、新しいはに開くとのことです。

According to the company announcement, the new branch will open in June.

Why this works

とのことです fits the formal company announcement and keeps the report concise.

からのメールでは、レポートのが延びたということだ。

The teacher's email says that the report deadline was extended.

Why this works

The email provides the source; the clause before ということだ gives the reported content.

ニュースによれば、空港はもう利用できるとのことです。

According to the news, the airport can already be used again.

Why this works

The combination marks an external source and reports its information in a formal register.

田中さんは少し遅れるとのことなので、先に始めましょう。

Mr. Tanaka says he will be a little late, so let's begin first.

Why this works

とのこと turns Tanaka's message into information that can support the following decision.

Guided checks

市の説明（　）、工事は月曜日からだということです。

メールによると、会議は中止（　）。

林さんは来週休む（　）、部長から聞きました。

Recap: Use these forms to pass on acquired information; choose ～とのことだ for a shorter, more formal report.

Present a general belief or received opinion

2. ～と言われている

Core meaning: Say that something is generally believed, commonly reported, or widely expected.

Formation

普通形 + と言われている

Nuance and use

The passive form shifts attention away from a single speaker. It is useful for social beliefs, reputations, scientific claims, traditions, and predictions that circulate among people.

Compare

～ということだ reports information from a source. ～と言われている presents the content as a broader received view, even when the exact source is unknown.

Common mistake

Do not use this form for a private statement made once by a clearly identified person; use ～と言った or a source-reporting form instead.

Examples

Book example

はがすると言われている。

People say that black clothing will be fashionable this year.

Why this works

と言われている presents a circulating prediction without identifying one particular speaker.

Book example

は体にいいと言われている。

Natto is said to be good for the body.

Why this works

The statement is treated as a widely received opinion rather than one person's direct claim.

Book example

今度のでは中川がするだろうと言われています。

It is said that Nakagawa will probably win the next tournament.

Why this works

The passive reporting form presents the prediction as what people generally expect.

この町の水はとてもきれいだと言われている。

The water in this town is said to be very clean.

Why this works

The sentence presents a general reputation shared about the town.

早寝早起きは健康にいいと言われています。

Going to bed and waking early is said to be good for one's health.

Why this works

The form introduces familiar health advice as a received view.

この山は、神様が住んでいたと言われている。

It is said that a god lived on this mountain long ago.

Why this works

The passive report suits a traditional story with no single current speaker.

来年は外国からの旅行者が増えると言われています。

It is said that the number of visitors from abroad will increase next year.

Why this works

A public prediction is presented without attaching it to one analyst.

この方法がいちばん安全だと言われているが、注意は必要だ。

This method is said to be the safest, but caution is still necessary.

Why this works

The general evaluation is acknowledged and then limited by the speaker.

では春が新しい生活の季節だと言われている。

In Japan, spring is said to be the season of new beginnings.

Why this works

The sentence describes a commonly shared cultural idea.

そのは十年に一人の才能だと言われている。

That athlete is said to be a once-in-a-decade talent.

Why this works

The form conveys a reputation repeated by many people.

Guided checks

この寺は千年前に建てられた（　）。

納豆は体にいい（　）。

来年は物価が上がるだろう（　）。

Recap: Use ～と言われている when the important point is that a view is broadly held or repeated.

Repeat hearsay without fully confirming it

3. ～とか

Core meaning: Mention information you heard when you are not presenting it as firmly verified.

Formation

普通形 + とか

Nuance and use

Sentence-final とか often means 'I hear...' or 'apparently...'. It deliberately leaves the source vague and can invite the listener to confirm or react.

Compare

～そうだ is a neutral hearsay form. ～とか sounds less certain and more conversational. Enumeration とか meaning 'things like' is a different use.

Common mistake

Keep the clause before とか in plain form. Do not add です directly before it as いいですとか when reporting the proposition 'it is good.'

Examples

Book example

まただとか。今度はどちらに行かれるんですか。

I hear you are going on another business trip next month. Where are you going this time?

Why this works

とか lightly repeats news the speaker heard and invites confirmation from the listener.

Book example

お宅ではいろいろなを飼っているとか。にぎやかでしょうね。

I hear you keep various animals at home. It must be lively.

Why this works

とか signals that the information is hearsay and that the speaker is not presenting it as verified fact.

Book example

あの店のパンはとてもおいしいとか。今日、帰りに買って帰ります。

I hear that shop's bread is very good. I will buy some on my way home today.

Why this works

The speaker bases a decision on a report whose source is left vague.

駅の近くに新しい図書館ができるとか。

I hear a new library will be built near the station.

Why this works

The speaker passes on news but leaves its source and certainty open.

山田さん、結婚するとか。おめでとうございます。

I hear you are getting married next month, Mr. Yamada. Congratulations.

Why this works

とか introduces personal news gently and gives the listener room to confirm it.

この薬は眠くなることがあるとか。運転はやめよう。

I hear this medicine can make you sleepy. I won't drive.

Why this works

The speaker acts cautiously on information that has not been presented as fully verified.

あの映画、海外ですごく人気があるとか。

Apparently that film is extremely popular overseas.

Why this works

とか conveys a casual report whose source is not specified.

の試験はかなり難しいとか。今から復習しよう。

I hear next week's test is quite difficult. I'll start reviewing now.

Why this works

The hearsay motivates a decision while remaining explicitly unconfirmed.

社長も会議に出るとか。本当ですか。

I hear the company president will also attend the meeting. Is that true?

Why this works

The follow-up question makes the speaker's uncertainty especially clear.

この店は夜遅くまで開いているとか。帰りに寄ってみない？

I hear this shop stays open late. Want to stop by on the way home?

Why this works

The report is casual and becomes the basis for a suggestion.

Guided checks

来月、駅前にホテルが開く（　）。

あの店のケーキはおいしい（　）。本当かな。

佐藤さんも参加する（　）。本人に聞いてみよう。

Recap: Use sentence-final ～とか for lightly held, unconfirmed hearsay.

Quote or relay information casually

4. ～って

Core meaning: Use a casual spoken form of quotation or hearsay in conversation.

Formation

普通形 + って（言っていた／聞いた／いうことだ may be omitted）

Nuance and use

って can mark quoted content before a reporting verb or stand at the end when that verb is understood. It is common among friends, family, and classmates, but unsuitable for formal writing.

Compare

～と is the neutral quotation particle. ～って is its casual conversational counterpart; ～とのことだ is much more formal.

Common mistake

Do not use casual ～って in formal reports or messages to people who require polite business language.

Examples

Book example

小川さん、今日は休むって言ってたよ。

Ogawa said that they would take today off.

Why this works

って casually marks the content of 言ってた in everyday speech.

Book example

佐藤さんの奥さんはのだって。

I heard that Ms. Sato's wife is a cooking teacher.

Why this works

The omitted reporting verb is understood from context; って alone passes on the casual report.

Book example

にタイのレストランができたんだって。行ってみようよ。

I heard a Thai restaurant opened in front of the station. Let's try it.

Why this works

んだって is a conversational way to share newly heard information before making a suggestion.

Book example

山川君、がまで来てくださいって。

Yamakawa, the teacher says to come to the staff room.

Why this works

って directly carries the teacher's requested words in a casual relay.

母が夕飯は七時だって言ってた。

Mom said dinner is at seven.

Why this works

って marks the content before the casual reporting verb 言ってた.

明日の授業、休みだって。

I heard tomorrow's class is canceled.

Why this works

The reporting verb is omitted because the hearsay meaning is clear in conversation.

田中君、あとでしてって。

Tanaka says to call him later.

Why this works

って relays the content of a casual request.

このアプリ、無料で使えるんだって。

I heard this app can be used for free.

Why this works

んだって shares newly learned information with conversational emphasis.

は何時に来るって言ってた？

What time did the teacher say they would come?

Why this works

って marks the quoted question content before 言ってた.

駅の西口で会おうって。

They said we should meet at the west exit of the station.

Why this works

The omitted speaker and verb are recoverable from the surrounding conversation.

Guided checks

明日の会議は九時からだ（　）。

先生が教室へ来てください（　）。

山田さんは休む（　）言ってたよ。

Recap: Use ～って to quote or pass on information naturally in casual speech.

Report information concisely in writing

5. ～という

Core meaning: State reported information in a concise, slightly written style.

Formation

普通形 + という

Nuance and use

Sentence-final という is common in historical notes, articles, explanations, and other writing. It reports information while keeping the prose compact.

Compare

It is close to hearsay ～そうだ, but ～という feels more written and explanatory. Do not confuse it with NというN, which names or defines a noun.

Common mistake

This sentence-final use needs a complete reported clause before という; it is not the same as leaving a noun-modifying phrase unfinished.

Examples

Book example

このは、広いだったという。

It is said that this area used to be a broad field.

Why this works

という gives a compact, slightly written report about local history.

Book example

このはで古くから行われてきたという。

It is said that this festival has been held in the village since long ago.

Why this works

The writer reports established background information without naming a present speaker.

Book example

は1300年ぐらい前にからに伝わったという。

It is said that tofu reached Japan from China around 1,300 years ago.

Why this works

という suits a concise historical statement in expository writing.

この橋は百年以上前に造られたという。

It is said that this bridge was built more than a hundred years ago.

Why this works

The form provides concise historical background in an expository register.

研究によると、朝の運動は集中力を高めるという。

Research says that morning exercise improves concentration.

Why this works

The source is named, and sentence-final という summarizes its finding compactly.

その島にはまだ知られていない植物が多いという。

It is said that the island has many plants that are still unknown.

Why this works

The writer presents reported factual information without quoting a speaker.

、この湖には大きな魚が住んでいたという。

It is said that a large fish once lived in this lake.

Why this works

The form suits a written account of a local story.

この制度を利用する学生は毎年増えているという。

It is reported that the number of students using this system increases every year.

Why this works

という condenses a reported trend in formal explanatory prose.

専門家は、来年景気が少し回復するという。

Experts say that the economy will recover somewhat next year.

Why this works

The complete proposition is reported concisely after an identified group source.

記録では、この寺は三度建て直されたという。

Records say that this temple was rebuilt three times.

Why this works

The records supply the evidence, and という gives the reported historical conclusion.

Guided checks

記録では、この城は四百年前に建てられた（　）。

研究によると、短い休憩は集中に役立つ（　）。

この祭りは江戸時代に始まった（　）。

Recap: Use sentence-final ～という for compact reported information, especially in written explanations.
