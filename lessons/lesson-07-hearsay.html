1. ～ということだ・～とのことだ

Formation

普通形 + ということだ／とのことだ（命令・依頼も引用形のまま）

Book explanation

～ということだ is neutral and explanatory. ～とのことだ is more concise and formal, so it appears often in messages, notices, and business communication. Add ～によると, ～によれば, or ～では when the source matters.

English study note

Core meaning: Report information you learned from another person, notice, email, or other source. Compare～そうだ also reports information but focuses less on the exact content or source. ～とのことだ sounds more formal than conversational ～って. Common mistakeDo not use the polite form inside the report merely because the whole sentence is polite. Report the original content in plain form, then add ということです or とのことです.

Book examples

Book example

のおらせによれば、このはからがということです。

According to the city notice, construction on this road will begin next week.

Why this works

市のお知らせによれば names the official source; ということです relays its information without quoting the notice directly.

Book example

ののでは、こののはとてもおいしいということだ。

According to someone at the shop, the rice from this region is very delicious.

Why this works

店の人の話では frames the report's source, and ということだ passes on the shop worker's statement.

Book example

さっきさんからがありました。はにれないとのことです。

Mr. Kawamura called a little while ago. He says that he cannot return to the office today.

Why this works

とのことです gives the content of the earlier telephone message in a concise, businesslike style.

Book example

メールによると、さんははとてもとのことです。

According to the email, Ms. Hayashi will be very busy next week.

Why this works

メールによると supplies a written source, while とのことです formally reports its content.

Book example

【】がまったとのこと、おめでとうございます。

[Letter] Congratulations on getting a new job.

Why this works

In a letter, とのこと can nominalize the news being acknowledged before the congratulatory message.

2. ～と言われている

Formation

普通形 + と言われている

Book explanation

The passive form shifts attention away from a single speaker. It is useful for social beliefs, reputations, scientific claims, traditions, and predictions that circulate among people.

English study note

Core meaning: Say that something is generally believed, commonly reported, or widely expected. Compare～ということだ reports information from a source. ～と言われている presents the content as a broader received view, even when the exact source is unknown. Common mistakeDo not use this form for a private statement made once by a clearly identified person; use ～と言った or a source-reporting form instead.

Book examples

Book example

はがするとわれて。

People say that black clothing will be fashionable this year.

Why this works

と言われている presents a circulating prediction without identifying one particular speaker.

Book example

はにいいとわれて。

Natto is said to be good for the body.

Why this works

The statement is treated as a widely received opinion rather than one person's direct claim.

Book example

のではがするだろうとわれています。

It is said that Nakagawa will probably win the next tournament.

Why this works

The passive reporting form presents the prediction as what people generally expect.

3. ～とか

Formation

普通形 + とか

Book explanation

Sentence-final とか often means 'I hear...' or 'apparently...'. It deliberately leaves the source vague and can invite the listener to confirm or react.

English study note

Core meaning: Mention information you heard when you are not presenting it as firmly verified. Compare～そうだ is a neutral hearsay form. ～とか sounds less certain and more conversational. Enumeration とか meaning 'things like' is a different use. Common mistakeKeep the clause before とか in plain form. Do not add です directly before it as いいですとか when reporting the proposition 'it is good.'

Book examples

Book example

まただとか。はどちらにかれるんですか。

I hear you are going on another business trip next month. Where are you going this time?

Why this works

とか lightly repeats news the speaker heard and invites confirmation from the listener.

Book example

ではなをとか。でしょうね。

I hear you keep various animals at home. It must be lively.

Why this works

とか signals that the information is hearsay and that the speaker is not presenting it as verified fact.

Book example

あののはとてもおいしいとか。、りに。

I hear that shop's bread is very good. I will buy some on my way home today.

Why this works

The speaker bases a decision on a report whose source is left vague.

4. ～って

Formation

普通形 + って（言っていた／聞いた／いうことだ may be omitted）

Book explanation

って can mark quoted content before a reporting verb or stand at the end when that verb is understood. It is common among friends, family, and classmates, but unsuitable for formal writing.

English study note

Core meaning: Use a casual spoken form of quotation or hearsay in conversation. Compare～と is the neutral quotation particle. ～って is its casual conversational counterpart; ～とのことだ is much more formal. Common mistakeDo not use casual ～って in formal reports or messages to people who require polite business language.

Book examples

Book example

さん、はってたよ。

Ogawa said that they would take today off.

Why this works

って casually marks the content of 言ってた in everyday speech.

Book example

さんのさんはのだって。

I heard that Ms. Sato's wife is a cooking teacher.

Why this works

The omitted reporting verb is understood from context; って alone passes on the casual report.

Book example

にタイのレストランができたんだって。ってみようよ。

I heard a Thai restaurant opened in front of the station. Let's try it.

Why this works

んだって is a conversational way to share newly heard information before making a suggestion.

Book example

、がまでくださいって。

Yamakawa, the teacher says to come to the staff room.

Why this works

って directly carries the teacher's requested words in a casual relay.

5. ～という

Formation

普通形 + という

Book explanation

Sentence-final という is common in historical notes, articles, explanations, and other writing. It reports information while keeping the prose compact.

English study note

Core meaning: State reported information in a concise, slightly written style. CompareIt is close to hearsay ～そうだ, but ～という feels more written and explanatory. Do not confuse it with NというN, which names or defines a noun. Common mistakeThis sentence-final use needs a complete reported clause before という; it is not the same as leaving a noun-modifying phrase unfinished.

Book examples

Book example

このは、いだったという。

It is said that this area used to be a broad field.

Why this works

という gives a compact, slightly written report about local history.

Book example

このはでからわれてきたという。

It is said that this festival has been held in the village since long ago.

Why this works

The writer reports established background information without naming a present speaker.

Book example

は1300ぐらいにからにという。

It is said that tofu reached Japan from China around 1,300 years ago.

Why this works

という suits a concise historical statement in expository writing.
