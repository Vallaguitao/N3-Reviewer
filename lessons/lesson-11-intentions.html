Make and maintain a personal decision

1. ～ことにする

Core meaning: The speaker deliberately decides what to do or not do.

Formation

V dictionary / Vない + ことにする・ことにした・ことにしている

Nuance and use

Use ことにした for one completed decision. Use ことにしている for a standing personal rule or repeated policy. The subject is normally the decision-maker, not an outside authority.

Compare

ことになる presents an arrangement or outcome decided by circumstances or other people; ことにする keeps the decision with the speaker or grammatical subject.

Common mistake

Do not use the potential form before ことにする when you mean a choice. Choose the action itself: 行くことにする, not 行けることにする.

Examples

Book example

がこわれたので、新しいのを買うことにした。

The refrigerator broke, so I decided to buy a new one.

Why this works

The plain action before ことにする is the option the subject deliberately selects.

Book example

と怒られるので、何も言わないでいることにした。

I would be scolded if I interfered, so I decided to say nothing and keep quiet.

Why this works

The plain action before ことにする is the option the subject deliberately selects.

Book example

Why this works

Make a continuing effort or habit

2. ～ようにする

Core meaning: Try to make a desired behavior or state happen regularly.

Formation

V dictionary / Vない + ようにする・ようにしている・ようにしてください

Nuance and use

This pattern focuses on effort, habit, or self-management rather than a single decision. ようにしている means the effort is already an ongoing routine.

Compare

ことにする is the decision itself; ようにする is the continuing effort to put that decision into practice.

Common mistake

Use the dictionary or ない form directly. Do not insert こと: × 食べることようにする.

Examples

Book example

水や電気はに使うようにしましょう。

Let us try to use water and electricity carefully.

Why this works

ようにする frames the action as a target the subject intentionally works to maintain.

Book example

妻「あなたの帰りが遅いから、たちがさびしがっているわよ。」夫「そうか。これからはもっと帰るようにするよ。」

Wife: The children miss you because you come home late every day. Husband: I see. From now on I will try to come home earlier.

Why this works

Please make sure not to be late for the meeting time.

Attempt an action or reach its starting point

3. ～（よ）うとする

Core meaning: Try to do something, be just about to do it, or show unwillingness with ～ようとしない.

Formation

V volitional + とする・としたとき・としたら / V volitional + としない

Nuance and use

With a deliberate person, it describes an attempt. With とき or たら, it often marks the instant an action is about to begin. The negative ～ようとしない means someone will not even try.

Compare

～てみる means actually trying an action to see the result; ～ようとする can stop at the attempt and may never reach completion.

Common mistake

Attach とする to the volitional form: 読もうとする, not 読むとする.

Examples

Book example

あの子はいっしょうけんめい、テーブルの上のおもちゃを取ろうとしている。

That child is stretching out with all their might, trying to take the toy on the table.

Why this works

The volitional form plus とする focuses on an attempted or imminent action, not guaranteed completion.

Book example

きのうのはとしてもなかなか。

Last night, even when I tried to sleep, I could not fall asleep easily.

Why this works

The volitional form plus とする focuses on an attempted or imminent action, not guaranteed completion.

Book example

Describe an assumption about oneself

4. ～つもり

Core meaning: Say that the subject believes they acted, behaved, or are regarded in a certain way, even if reality differs.

Formation

Vた / Vている / Nの + つもり; な-adjective + な + つもり

Nuance and use

In this lesson, つもり is not merely a future plan. It expresses the subject's self-image or claimed intention, often contrasted by が, のに, or a surprised question.

Compare

V dictionary + つもりだ can state a future plan. Vた + つもりだ often means 'believing that one has done it,' which may conflict with the actual result.

Common mistake

Read the surrounding contrast. 掃除したつもり does not guarantee that the room is actually clean.

Examples

Book example

わたしは77歳ですが、まだまだつもりです。

I am 77 this year, but I still think of myself as young.

Why this works

つもり presents the subject's own belief or intended interpretation, while the rest of the sentence tests it against reality.

Book example

じょうだんで言ったつもりのだったが、はような顔をした。

I meant the words as a joke, but he looked angry.

Why this works

つもり presents the subject's own belief or intended interpretation, while the rest of the sentence tests it against reality.
