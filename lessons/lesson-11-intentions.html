1. ～ことにする

Formation

V dictionary / Vない + ことにする・ことにした・ことにしている

Book explanation

Use ことにした for one completed decision. Use ことにしている for a standing personal rule or repeated policy. The subject is normally the decision-maker, not an outside authority.

English study note

Core meaning: The speaker deliberately decides what to do or not do. Compareことになる presents an arrangement or outcome decided by circumstances or other people; ことにする keeps the decision with the speaker or grammatical subject. Common mistakeDo not use the potential form before ことにする when you mean a choice. Choose the action itself: 行くことにする, not 行けることにする.

Book examples

Book example

がこわれたので、のをことにした。

The refrigerator broke, so I decided to buy a new one.

Why this works

The plain action before ことにする is the option the subject deliberately selects.

Book example

とので、もでいることにした。

I would be scolded if I interfered, so I decided to say nothing and keep quiet.

Why this works

The plain action before ことにする is the option the subject deliberately selects.

Book example

「、のにディズニーランドにいってよ。」「よし、わかった。じゃ、とゴルフにはことにするよ。」

Daughter: Dad, take me to Disneyland next holiday. Father: All right. Then I will cancel my promise to play golf with my friends.

Why this works

The plain action before ことにする is the option the subject deliberately selects.

Book example

のはいつもとてもきびしいが、わたしはことにしている。

The manager's words are always harsh, but I make it a rule not to let them bother me.

Why this works

The plain action before ことにする is the option the subject deliberately selects.

2. ～ようにする

Formation

V dictionary / Vない + ようにする・ようにしている・ようにしてください

Book explanation

This pattern focuses on effort, habit, or self-management rather than a single decision. ようにしている means the effort is already an ongoing routine.

English study note

Core meaning: Try to make a desired behavior or state happen regularly. Compareことにする is the decision itself; ようにする is the continuing effort to put that decision into practice. Common mistakeUse the dictionary or ない form directly. Do not insert こと: × 食べることようにする.

Book examples

Book example

やはにようにしましょう。

Let us try to use water and electricity carefully.

Why this works

ようにする frames the action as a target the subject intentionally works to maintain.

Book example

「あなたのがから、たちがさびしがっているわよ。」「そうか。これからはもっとようにするよ。」

Wife: The children miss you because you come home late every day. Husband: I see. From now on I will try to come home earlier.

Why this works

ようにする frames the action as a target the subject intentionally works to maintain.

Book example

にようにしてください。

Please make sure not to be late for the meeting time.

Why this works

ようにする frames the action as a target the subject intentionally works to maintain.

Book example

わたしはなるべくでをようにしている。

I try to cook and eat my own meals whenever possible.

Why this works

ようにする frames the action as a target the subject intentionally works to maintain.

3. ～（よ）うとする

Formation

V volitional + とする・としたとき・としたら / V volitional + としない

Book explanation

With a deliberate person, it describes an attempt. With とき or たら, it often marks the instant an action is about to begin. The negative ～ようとしない means someone will not even try.

English study note

Core meaning: Try to do something, be just about to do it, or show unwillingness with ～ようとしない. Compare～てみる means actually trying an action to see the result; ～ようとする can stop at the attempt and may never reach completion. Common mistakeAttach とする to the volitional form: 読もうとする, not 読むとする.

Book examples

Book example

あのはいっしょうけんめい、テーブルののおもちゃをとしている。

That child is stretching out with all their might, trying to take the toy on the table.

Why this works

The volitional form plus とする focuses on an attempted or imminent action, not guaranteed completion.

Book example

きのうのはとしてもなかなか。

Last night, even when I tried to sleep, I could not fall asleep easily.

Why this works

The volitional form plus とする focuses on an attempted or imminent action, not guaranteed completion.

Book example

をとしたとき、が。

Just as I was about to leave the house, it suddenly began to pour.

Why this works

The volitional form plus とする focuses on an attempted or imminent action, not guaranteed completion.

Book example

をとしたら、がなってしまった。

When I tried to lift the heavy luggage, my lower back started to hurt.

Why this works

The volitional form plus とする focuses on an attempted or imminent action, not guaranteed completion.

Book example

いくらもはへとしない。

No matter how much I urge him, my husband will not try to go to the hospital.

Why this works

The volitional form plus とする focuses on an attempted or imminent action, not guaranteed completion.

Book example

にをしようとしたが、はをとはしなかった。

I tried to explain the circumstances to my father, but he would not listen.

Why this works

The volitional form plus とする focuses on an attempted or imminent action, not guaranteed completion.

4. ～つもり

Formation

Vた / Vている / Nの + つもり; な-adjective + な + つもり

Book explanation

In this lesson, つもり is not merely a future plan. It expresses the subject's self-image or claimed intention, often contrasted by が, のに, or a surprised question.

English study note

Core meaning: Say that the subject believes they acted, behaved, or are regarded in a certain way, even if reality differs. CompareV dictionary + つもりだ can state a future plan. Vた + つもりだ often means 'believing that one has done it,' which may conflict with the actual result. Common mistakeRead the surrounding contrast. 掃除したつもり does not guarantee that the room is actually clean.

Book examples

Book example

わたしは77ですが、まだまだつもりです。

I am 77 this year, but I still think of myself as young.

Why this works

つもり presents the subject's own belief or intended interpretation, while the rest of the sentence tests it against reality.

Book example

じょうだんでつもりのだったが、はようなをした。

I meant the words as a joke, but he looked angry.

Why this works

つもり presents the subject's own belief or intended interpretation, while the rest of the sentence tests it against reality.

Book example

にしたはのつもりらしいが、はわたしのがなのだ。

Yumi, who joined the company first, seems to think she is my senior, but I am actually older.

Why this works

つもり presents the subject's own belief or intended interpretation, while the rest of the sentence tests it against reality.

Book example

こんなにのに、それでもしたつもりですか。

It is this dirty, and you still consider it cleaned?

Why this works

つもり presents the subject's own belief or intended interpretation, while the rest of the sentence tests it against reality.
