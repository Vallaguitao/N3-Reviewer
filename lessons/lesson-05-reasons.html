1. ～ためだ・～ため（に）…

Formation

Nの／V／イAい／ナAな + ためだ・ため（に）

Book explanation

Compared with から and ので, causal ～ため sounds more written and objective. It often appears in notices, reports, and formal explanations.

English study note

Core meaning: Use ～ためだ or ～ために to present an objective, often formal cause. The result is commonly factual or unwanted. CompareUse から for a personal reason and ので for a softer explanation. Purpose ～ために has an intentional action after it; causal ～ために presents a result. Common mistakeDo not normally put a request, command, or direct intention after causal ～ために.

Book examples

Book example

にミスがのは、よくをしなかったためだろう。

The report probably had many mistakes because it was not reviewed carefully.

Why this works

～ためだ gives the likely formal cause of an undesirable result.

Book example

このにはがいないために、のときはとなりのまでばならない。

Because there is no doctor in this village, residents must go to the next town when they are ill.

Why this works

The lack of a doctor objectively causes the inconvenience that follows.

Book example

のため、のはさせていただきます。

Because of a business trip, I will be absent from tomorrow's meeting.

Why this works

Nのため gives a concise formal reason for an absence.

2. ～によって…・～による

Formation

N + によって／N + による + N

Book explanation

Cause によって often describes events beyond a person's control. Means によって focuses on the method or channel used to achieve something.

English study note

Core meaning: Use Nによって for a formal cause or for the means by which a result becomes possible. Use Nによる before a noun. CompareFor an everyday instrument, で is usually simpler. によって is useful when the method itself is important or the wording is formal. Common mistakeDo not confuse によって with によると or によれば, which cite an information source.

Book examples

Book example

うちのでは、によってたたみのはもうできなくなった。

At our factory, tatami production is no longer possible because of a material shortage.

Why this works

The shortage is presented as the formal cause of the production stoppage.

Book example

のインフルエンザは、までにないのウイルスによるものである。

This year's influenza is caused by a type of virus not seen before.

Why this works

による modifies もの and identifies the cause.

Book example

をことによってそののたちのもことができる。

By learning a foreign language, one can also understand how people in that country think.

Why this works

Learning the language is the means that makes understanding possible.

Book example

クレジットカードによるおをされるは、のをおください。

Those who wish to pay by credit card should read the following notes.

Why this works

による modifies payment and identifies the formal method.

3. ～から…・～ことから…

Formation

N + から／ + ことから

Book explanation

ことから often sounds analytical: the speaker looks at evidence and explains what followed or what can be inferred.

English study note

Core meaning: Use Nから for a starting source and ～ことから when a fact or event becomes the basis of a development, name, or conclusion. Compareから after a clause gives an ordinary personal reason. Nから and ことから here focus on the origin or evidential basis. Common mistakeDo not use ことから merely as a casual replacement for every sentence-final から.

Book examples

Book example

わずかなからとのがなってしまった。

A slight misunderstanding caused my relationship with a friend to deteriorate.

Why this works

Nから marks the starting cause from which the bad relationship developed.

Book example

のでとなりのになったことから、わたしたちはしくなった。

We became close after being seated next to each other in Japanese class.

Why this works

ことから identifies the event that began the relationship.

Book example

がよくことから、はだとすぐにわかった。

Because their faces look very similar, I immediately knew they were parent and child.

Why this works

The facial resemblance is evidence for the conclusion.

4. ～おかげだ・～おかげで…／～せいだ・～せいで…

Formation

Nの／ + おかげだ・おかげで／せいだ・せいで

Book explanation

These patterns do more than state causation: they reveal the speaker's positive or negative evaluation of the cause.

English study note

Core meaning: Use おかげ for a cause the speaker appreciates and せい for a cause blamed for an unfavorable result. Compareため and によって can stay neutral and objective. おかげ and せい explicitly assign credit or blame. Common mistakeMatch the evaluation to the outcome. An obviously favorable result normally takes おかげ; an unfavorable one normally takes せい.

Book examples

Book example

いいにがのはのおかげです。ありがとうございました。

It is thanks to my teacher that I obtained a job at a good company. Thank you very much.

Why this works

おかげ credits the teacher for the favorable outcome.

Book example

のいいがおかげで、が。

Thanks to the continued fine weather, the construction finished early.

Why this works

Good weather is positively evaluated as the helpful cause.

Book example

せいで、かなりいる。

Because I have been busy lately, I am quite tired.

Why this works

せいで assigns blame for the speaker's tiredness.

5. ～のだから…

Formation

+ のだから／ナA・N + なのだから／では んだから

Book explanation

The speaker expects the listener to accept the reason. This can sound persuasive, encouraging, admonishing, or insistent.

English study note

Core meaning: Use ～のだから when the reason is treated as known, obvious, or already established, then draw a judgment, request, warning, or intention from it. ComparePlain から simply gives a reason. のだから frames that reason as shared or undeniable background. Common mistakeBecause the pattern can sound forceful, be careful when correcting or pressuring someone of higher status.

Book examples

Book example

はのだから、いろいろながあるのはだ。

The world is large, so it is natural that many different customs exist.

Why this works

The wide world is treated as an established fact supporting the judgment.

Book example

あなたはけがをしているんだから、をしてはいけませんよ。

You are injured, so you must not overdo it.

Why this works

The known injury supports a warning not to overdo things.

Book example

ください。にやっているんですから。

Please do not laugh. I am doing this seriously.

Why this works

んですから emphasizes the already-relevant reason for the request.
