State a formal cause or reason

1. ～ためだ・～ため（に）…

Core meaning: Use ～ためだ or ～ために to present an objective, often formal cause. The result is commonly factual or unwanted.

Formation

Nの／V普通形／イAい／ナAな + ためだ・ため（に）

Nuance and use

Compared with から and ので, causal ～ため sounds more written and objective. It often appears in notices, reports, and formal explanations.

Compare

Use から for a personal reason and ので for a softer explanation. Purpose ～ために has an intentional action after it; causal ～ために presents a result.

Common mistake

Do not normally put a request, command, or direct intention after causal ～ために.

Examples

Book example

にミスが多かったのは、よくをしなかったためだろう。

The report probably had many mistakes because it was not reviewed carefully.

Why this works

～ためだ gives the likely formal cause of an undesirable result.

Book example

この村にはがいないために、のときはとなりの町まで行かなければならない。

Because there is no doctor in this village, residents must go to the next town when they are ill.

Why this works

The lack of a doctor objectively causes the inconvenience that follows.

Book example

のため、明日のはさせていただきます。

Because of a business trip, I will be absent from tomorrow's meeting.

Why this works

Nのため gives a concise formal reason for an absence.

雨のため、試合は中止になった。

The match was canceled because of the rain.

Why this works

This example applies ～ためだ・～ため（に）… in a practical context.

のため、この道は通れません。

This road is closed because of construction.

Why this works

Show a cause, means, or method

2. ～によって…・～による

Core meaning: Use Nによって for a formal cause or for the means by which a result becomes possible. Use Nによる before a noun.

Formation

N + によって／N + による + N

Nuance and use

Cause によって often describes events beyond a person's control. Means によって focuses on the method or channel used to achieve something.

Compare

For an everyday instrument, で is usually simpler. によって is useful when the method itself is important or the wording is formal.

Common mistake

Do not confuse によって with によると or によれば, which cite an information source.

Examples

Book example

うちのでは、によってたたみのはもうできなくなった。

At our factory, tatami production is no longer possible because of a material shortage.

Why this works

The shortage is presented as the formal cause of the production stoppage.

Book example

今年のインフルエンザは、今までにない型のウイルスによるものである。

This year's influenza is caused by a type of virus not seen before.

Why this works

による modifies もの and identifies the cause.

Book example

を学ぶことによってその国の人たちのも知ることができる。

By learning a foreign language, one can also understand how people in that country think.

Why this works

Learning the language is the means that makes understanding possible.

Book example

クレジットカードによるおをされる方は、次のをお読みください。

Those who wish to pay by credit card should read the following notes.

Why this works

による modifies payment and identifies the formal method.

地震によって多くの建物が壊れた。

Many buildings were damaged by the earthquake.

Why this works

The disease can be detected early through a new testing method.

Why this works

Why this works

This example shows ～によって…・～による in a distinct everyday context.

Why this works

This example shows ～によって…・～による in a distinct everyday context.

Show an origin or evidence-based cause

3. ～から…・～ことから…

Core meaning: Use Nから for a starting source and ～ことから when a fact or event becomes the basis of a development, name, or conclusion.

Formation

N + から／普通形 + ことから

Nuance and use

ことから often sounds analytical: the speaker looks at evidence and explains what followed or what can be inferred.

Compare

から after a clause gives an ordinary personal reason. Nから and ことから here focus on the origin or evidential basis.

Common mistake

Do not use ことから merely as a casual replacement for every sentence-final から.

Examples

Book example

わずかなから友だちとの関係が悪くなってしまった。

A slight misunderstanding caused my relationship with a friend to deteriorate.

Why this works

Nから marks the starting cause from which the bad relationship developed.

Book example

日本語の授業でとなりの席になったことから、わたしたちは親しくなった。

We became close after being seated next to each other in Japanese class.

Why this works

ことから identifies the event that began the relationship.

Book example

顔がよく似ていることから、二人はだとすぐにわかった。

Because their faces look very similar, I immediately knew they were parent and child.

Why this works

The facial resemblance is evidence for the conclusion.

小さな不から大きな事故が起きた。

A small oversight led to a major accident.

Why this works

This example shows ～から…・～ことから… in a distinct everyday context.

一通のメールから、わたしたちの交流が始まった。

Our correspondence began with a single email.

Why this works

This example shows ～から…・～ことから… in a distinct everyday context.

窓がぬれていることから、夜に雨が降ったとわかる。

From the wet window, we can tell that it rained at night.

Why this works

This example shows ～から…・～ことから… in a distinct everyday context.

多くの古い道具が見つかったことから、ここに村があったと考えられる。

Because many old tools were found, it is thought that a village once stood here.

Why this works

This example shows ～から…・～ことから… in a distinct everyday context.

名前の形から、これは外国の会社だと思った。

From the form of the name, I thought it was a foreign company.

Why this works

This example shows ～から…・～ことから… in a distinct everyday context.

毎日の会話から少しずつが解けていった。

Through daily conversations, the misunderstanding gradually cleared up.

Why this works

This example shows ～から…・～ことから… in a distinct everyday context.

で同じ趣味を持ったことから、一緒に出かけるようになった。

Sharing the same hobby led the parent and child to go out together.

Why this works

This example shows ～から…・～ことから… in a distinct everyday context.

Guided checks

小さな不注意（　）事故が起きた。

顔が似ている（　）、親子だとわかった。

Recap: Use Nから for a starting source and ～ことから when a fact or event becomes the basis of a development, name, or conclusion.

Evaluate a helpful or harmful cause

4. ～おかげだ・～おかげで…／～せいだ・～せいで…

Core meaning: Use おかげ for a cause the speaker appreciates and せい for a cause blamed for an unfavorable result.

Formation

Nの／普通形 + おかげだ・おかげで／せいだ・せいで

Nuance and use

These patterns do more than state causation: they reveal the speaker's positive or negative evaluation of the cause.

Compare

ため and によって can stay neutral and objective. おかげ and せい explicitly assign credit or blame.

Common mistake

Match the evaluation to the outcome. An obviously favorable result normally takes おかげ; an unfavorable one normally takes せい.

Examples

Book example

いい会社にが決まったのは先生のおかげです。ありがとうございました。

It is thanks to my teacher that I obtained a job at a good company. Thank you very much.

Why this works

おかげ credits the teacher for the favorable outcome.

Book example

天気のいい日が続いたおかげで、が早く終わった。

Thanks to the continued fine weather, the construction finished early.

Why this works

Good weather is positively evaluated as the helpful cause.

Book example

最近忙しかったせいで、かなり疲れている。

Because I have been busy lately, I am quite tired.

Why this works

せいで assigns blame for the speaker's tiredness.

友だちのおかげで、迷わず会場に着けた。

Thanks to my friend, I reached the venue without getting lost.

Why this works

This example shows ～おかげだ・～おかげで…／～せいだ・～せいで… in a distinct everyday context.

毎日練習したおかげで、試合に勝てた。

Thanks to practicing every day, I won the match.

Why this works

This example shows ～おかげだ・～おかげで…／～せいだ・～せいで… in a distinct everyday context.

薬を飲んだおかげで、熱が下がった。

Thanks to the medicine, my fever went down.

Why this works

This example shows ～おかげだ・～おかげで…／～せいだ・～せいで… in a distinct everyday context.

寝坊したせいで、電車に乗り遅れた。

Because I overslept, I missed the train.

Why this works

This example shows ～おかげだ・～おかげで…／～せいだ・～せいで… in a distinct everyday context.

雨のせいで、楽しみにしていた試合が中止になった。

Because of the rain, the match I was looking forward to was canceled.

Why this works

This example shows ～おかげだ・～おかげで…／～せいだ・～せいで… in a distinct everyday context.

説明が足りなかったせいで、が生まれた。

Because the explanation was insufficient, a misunderstanding arose.

Why this works

This example shows ～おかげだ・～おかげで…／～せいだ・～せいで… in a distinct everyday context.

先生の助言のおかげで、のない計画ができた。

Thanks to the teacher's advice, I made a manageable plan.

Why this works

This example shows ～おかげだ・～おかげで…／～せいだ・～せいで… in a distinct everyday context.

Guided checks

先生の助言（　）、合格できた。

寝坊した（　）、遅刻した。

説明不足（　）、誤解された。

Recap: Use おかげ for a cause the speaker appreciates and せい for a cause blamed for an unfavorable result.

Reason from an established fact

5. ～のだから…

Core meaning: Use ～のだから when the reason is treated as known, obvious, or already established, then draw a judgment, request, warning, or intention from it.

Formation

普通形 + のだから／ナA・N + なのだから／会話では んだから

Nuance and use

The speaker expects the listener to accept the reason. This can sound persuasive, encouraging, admonishing, or insistent.

Compare

Plain から simply gives a reason. のだから frames that reason as shared or undeniable background.

Common mistake

Because the pattern can sound forceful, be careful when correcting or pressuring someone of higher status.

Examples

Book example

世界は広いのだから、いろいろながあるのはだ。

The world is large, so it is natural that many different customs exist.

Why this works

The wide world is treated as an established fact supporting the judgment.

Book example

あなたはけがをしているんだから、をしてはいけませんよ。

You are injured, so you must not overdo it.

Why this works

The known injury supports a warning not to overdo things.

Book example

笑わないでください。にやっているんですから。

Please do not laugh. I am doing this seriously.

Why this works

んですから emphasizes the already-relevant reason for the request.

明日は試験なのだから、今夜は早く寝よう。

Since the exam is tomorrow, let's go to bed early tonight.

Why this works

This example shows ～のだから… in a distinct everyday context.

約束したのだから、最後まで責任を持ってください。

Since you promised, please take responsibility to the end.

Why this works

This example shows ～のだから… in a distinct everyday context.

まだ時間があるのだから、あわてなくてもいい。

Since there is still time, you do not need to rush.

Why this works

This example shows ～のだから… in a distinct everyday context.

日本に住んでいるんだから、日本語を使ってみよう。

Since you live in Japan, try using Japanese.

Why this works

This example shows ～のだから… in a distinct everyday context.

大切ななのだから、準備を確認しておこう。

Since this is an important meeting, let's check the preparations.

Why this works

This example shows ～のだから… in a distinct everyday context.

なのだから、今日はをしないでください。

Since you are ill, please do not overdo it today.

Why this works

This example shows ～のだから… in a distinct everyday context.

自分で決めたのだから、に取り組みたい。

Since I decided this myself, I want to approach it seriously.

Why this works

This example shows ～のだから… in a distinct everyday context.

Guided checks

もう大人（　）、自分で決めなさい。

試験が近い（　）、復習しよう。

約束した（　）、守ってください。

Recap: Use ～のだから when the reason is treated as known, obvious, or already established, then draw a judgment, request, warning, or intention from it.
