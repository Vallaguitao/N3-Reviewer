Give or report a forceful order

1. 命令（しろ）／禁止（～な）

Core meaning: Order someone to act with the imperative, or forbid an action with the dictionary form plus な.

Formation

命令：動詞I う段→え段／動詞II ます→ろ／する→しろ／来る→来い
禁止：動辞書形 + な

Nuance and use

These forms are strong and can sound rough in direct conversation. They are common in emergencies, sports cheering, commands to animals, signs, quoted speech, and indirect reports such as 止まれという意味.

Compare

走れ is much stronger than 走ってください. 走るな forbids running; 走らないでください makes the prohibition polite.

Common mistake

For prohibition, attach な to the dictionary form: 入るな, not 入らないな.

Examples

Book example

【試合で】監督「走れ、走れ！」

During the match, the coach shouts, “Run! Run!”

Why this works

The sports setting licenses the forceful imperative as urgent encouragement.

Book example

犬に「降りろ。」と命令した。犬は命令に従った。

I ordered the dog, “Get down.” The dog obeyed.

Why this works

A direct command to an animal naturally uses the strong imperative.

Book example

赤信号は止まれという意味です。

A red light means “stop.”

Why this works

The command is quoted indirectly as the meaning of the signal.

Book example

引っ越しを手伝ってくれと友だちに頼んでみよう。

I will try asking a friend, “Help me move.”

Why this works

The plain command is embedded as the content of a request to a close friend.

Book example

立て札に「スピードを出すな！」と書いてある。

The sign says, “Do not speed!”

Why this works

A public warning sign uses the concise prohibitive form.

Book example

父は医者にお酒を飲むなと言われている。

My father has been told by his doctor not to drink alcohol.

Why this works

The prohibition is reported indirectly after 言われている.

危ない！　そこから離れろ！

Danger! Get away from there!

Why this works

An emergency makes a direct imperative appropriate.

試験中は辞書を見るな。

Do not look at a dictionary during the test.

Why this works

The dictionary form 見る plus な creates a strict prohibition.

先生は学生に静かにしろと言った。

The teacher told the students to be quiet.

Why this works

The imperative is embedded as reported command content.

あきらめるな。最後までがんばれ。

Do not give up. Keep going to the end.

Why this works

Sports-style encouragement pairs a prohibition with an imperative.

Guided checks

危ないから、そこに（　）！

監督は選手にもっと速く（　）と言った。

標識の「止まれ」は（　）形です。

Recap: Treat these as high-strength forms; direct them at people only where urgency, authority, convention, or quotation makes that force appropriate.

State a written rule or instruction

2. ～こと

Core meaning: Give an instruction, rule, caution, or prohibition in a concise written style.

Formation

動辞書形／ない形 + こと

Nuance and use

Sentence-final こと turns the clause into an impersonal rule. It appears in classroom instructions, notices, schedules, manuals, and posted cautions.

Compare

提出すること is a formal instruction. 提出してください directly and politely asks a person. 提出しろ is a forceful spoken command.

Common mistake

Do not add だ after the rule ending. 入らないこと is the instruction; 入らないことだ sounds like explanatory advice.

Examples

Book example

【学校で】先生「レポートは来週月曜日に必ず出すこと。遅れないこと。」

At school, the teacher says: “Submit the report next Monday without fail. Do not be late.”

Why this works

The teacher states concise institutional instructions.

Book example

申込書を書く前に注意書きをよく読むこと。

Read the instructions carefully before filling out the application.

Why this works

The sentence gives an impersonal written procedure.

Book example

【立て札】危ないからこの川で泳がないこと。

Sign: Do not swim in this river because it is dangerous.

Why this works

The negative form plus こと creates a posted prohibition.

試験中は携帯電話を使わないこと。

Do not use mobile phones during the examination.

Why this works

The rule is written as an impersonal prohibition.

図書館の本は二週間以内に返すこと。

Return library books within two weeks.

Why this works

こと states the borrowing rule concisely.

集合時間を必ず守ること。

Be sure to observe the meeting time.

Why this works

The ending frames compliance as a group instruction.

薬は一日三回、食後に飲むこと。

Take the medicine three times a day after meals.

Why this works

This reads like a concise written direction.

このドアを開けたままにしないこと。

Do not leave this door open.

Why this works

The negative rule targets the resulting state.

質問がある場合は担当者に連絡すること。

If you have questions, contact the person in charge.

Why this works

The condition is followed by a manual-like instruction.

部屋を出る前に窓を閉めること。

Close the windows before leaving the room.

Why this works

The sentence communicates a standing procedure rather than a one-time request.

Guided checks

【注意】ここに自転車を（　）こと。

レポートは金曜日までに（　）こと。

書き言葉の規則に最も合うのは（　）。

Recap: Final こと turns a dictionary-form or negative-form clause into an impersonal instruction.

State what is proper, expected, or regretted

3. ～べきだ・～べき／～べきではない

Core meaning: Express the speaker's judgment that an action ought to be done, ought not to be done, or should have been done.

Formation

動辞書形 + べきだ・べきではない
動辞書形 + べき + 名詞
過去の後悔：動辞書形 + べきだった／べきではなかった
する → するべき／すべき

Nuance and use

べき states a moral, practical, or common-sense judgment. It is the speaker's view, not necessarily a formal rule. Negative べきではない criticizes an action; past forms express regret or criticism after the fact.

Compare

～たほうがいい usually sounds like advice. ～べきだ is stronger and presents the action as the proper course. ～なければならない states necessity rather than judgment.

Common mistake

Negate the judgment, not the verb: 借りるべきではない, not 借りないべきだ.

Examples

Book example

これは大事なことですから、もう少し話し合ってから決めるべきだと思いますよ。

This is important, so I think we should discuss it more before deciding.

Why this works

The speaker judges more discussion to be the proper course.

Book example

仕事はたくさんあるが、まず、今日中にやるべきことから始めよう。

There is a lot of work, but let us start with what should be done today.

Why this works

べき modifies こと to identify the tasks that properly take priority.

Book example

せっかく入った会社なのだから、簡単に辞めるべきではない。

Since you made the effort to join the company, you should not quit so easily.

Why this works

The negative form criticizes quitting without sufficient thought.

Book example

子どもは夜遅くまで外にいるべきではない。

Children should not stay outside late at night.

Why this works

The speaker makes a normative judgment about appropriate behavior.

Book example

あしたまでのレポートがまだ書き終わらない。もっと早くから始めるべきだった。

I still have not finished tomorrow's report. I should have started earlier.

Why this works

べきだった looks back on the action not taken and expresses regret.

約束したことは守るべきだ。

You should keep what you promised.

Why this works

The speaker presents promise-keeping as the proper conduct.

人の秘密を勝手に話すべきではない。

You should not reveal another person's secret without permission.

Why this works

べきではない strongly criticizes the proposed behavior.

旅行の前に保険に入っておくべきです。

You should arrange insurance before traveling.

Why this works

The judgment is practical and preventative.

あの時、もっと正直に謝るべきだった。

I should have apologized more honestly then.

Why this works

The past form expresses regret about the missed action.

確認せずに送るべきではなかった。

I should not have sent it without checking.

Why this works

べきではなかった criticizes an action that was actually completed.

Guided checks

大切な問題だから、みんなで話し合う（　）だ。

友だちにうそをつく（　）。

電車に間に合わなかった。もっと早く出る（　）。

Recap: Use べき for the speaker's strong judgment, べきではない for criticism, and the past forms for regret after the outcome.

Offer advice—or challenge someone for not acting

4. ～たらどうか

Core meaning: Suggest that another person do something; depending on tone and context, criticize them for not having done it.

Formation

動た形 + ら + どうですか・どうでしょうか・どう？

Nuance and use

～たらどうでしょうか is comparatively gentle and polite. ～たらどう？ is casual. With blame, contrast, or a sharp tone, the same pattern can mean “Why don't you…?” and sound critical.

Compare

～といいですよ presents a generally good action. ～たらどうですか actively proposes a choice to the listener. ～べきだ is stronger and judges the action proper.

Common mistake

Use the past conditional base: 休んだらどう, not 休むたらどう. Also watch tone; the form is not automatically gentle.

Examples

Book example

体のことが心配なら、一度健康診断を受けたらどうでしょうか。

If you are worried about your health, why not have a medical examination once?

Why this works

The polite ending offers a considerate recommendation.

Book example

疲れているみたいですね。少し休んだらどうですか。

You look tired. Why don't you rest for a while?

Why this works

The suggestion responds directly to visible tiredness.

Book example

迷惑メールが多いの？　じゃ、アドレスを変えたらどう？

Are you getting a lot of spam? Then how about changing your address?

Why this works

The casual form proposes a practical solution between familiar speakers.

Book example

悪いのはそっちですよ。一言謝ったらどうですか。

You are the one at fault. Why don't you apologize?

Why this works

The blame in the first sentence makes the suggestion critical rather than gentle.

眠れないなら、温かいミルクを飲んだらどうですか。

If you cannot sleep, why not drink some warm milk?

Why this works

The speaker proposes a manageable remedy.

電車が混んでいるなら、一本待ったらどう？

If the train is crowded, how about waiting for the next one?

Why this works

The casual question offers an alternative action.

わからないことは先生に相談したらどうでしょうか。

Why not consult the teacher about what you do not understand?

Why this works

どうでしょうか makes the recommendation polite.

そんなに文句を言うなら、自分でやったらどう。

If you complain that much, why don't you do it yourself?

Why this works

The challenge and casual ending make the suggestion confrontational.

一人で決められないなら、家族に聞いたらどうですか。

If you cannot decide alone, why not ask your family?

Why this works

The suggestion addresses the listener's decision problem.

約束を忘れたのはあなたでしょう。すぐ連絡したらどうですか。

You are the one who forgot the promise. Why don't you contact them immediately?

Why this works

The accusatory context gives the recommendation a critical edge.

Guided checks

疲れているなら、少し（　）どうですか。

丁寧な提案に最も合うのは（　）。

「悪いのは君だ。謝ったらどう？」の調子は（　）。

Recap: The grammar proposes an action, but context and tone determine whether it feels helpful, casual, challenging, or critical.
