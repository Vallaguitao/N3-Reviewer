Show how extreme something is

～くらい／～ぐらい／～ほど

to the extent that; so…that; about as…as

Formation

N／V普通形／イA普通形／ナAな ＋ くらい・ぐらい・ほど · N ＋ くらいの／ほどの ＋ N

This pattern makes a degree vivid by giving a concrete result or comparison. The clause before it supplies the image: so delicious that you want it daily, so painful that standing is impossible, or wind about as strong as a typhoon.

ぐらい is common in conversation, while くらい is slightly more neutral in writing. ほど often sounds more measured or emphatic, but the choice depends on the expression; do not treat it as an automatic stronger version.

What to listen for

An observable result that lets the listener imagine the strength, amount, difficulty, or intensity.

Compare with ～ば～ほど

～くらい／～ほど gives one degree or illustrative limit. The Lesson 2 pattern ～ば～ほど links two changing scales: the more X changes, the more Y changes.

Common mistake

Do not place ほど after a result that has no meaningful degree. The example must help the listener measure or imagine the state.

Examples and scenarios

のはおいしい。くらいだ。

The bread at this shop is delicious—so delicious that I want to eat it every day.

Why this works

Wanting it every day provides an easy-to-imagine degree of deliciousness.

よう子さんのはなくらい。

Yoko's arms are so thin that they look as if they might break.

Why this works

The imagined result 折れそう shows an extreme visual degree.

によると、はぐらいのが吹くそうだ。

According to the forecast, today's wind may be about as strong as a typhoon.

Why this works

A typhoon is used as a noun-sized comparison for the wind's degree.

をさすほどではないが、少しがいる。

It is raining a little, though not enough to need an umbrella.

Why this works

ほどではない sets a degree below the practical umbrella threshold.

いられないほどのをに感じた。

I suddenly felt pain in my back so severe that I could not remain standing.

Why this works

Being unable to stand measures the pain's severity.

さっきがあった。が倒れるかと思うほど。

There was an earthquake a moment ago. It shook so violently that I thought the bookshelf might fall.

Why this works

The feared result gives a vivid measure of the shaking.

は、となりの部屋までが聞こえるくらい大きく。

The baby cried loudly enough to be heard in the next room.

Why this works

The distance at which the voice was heard measures the loudness.

このはでも持てるほど。

This luggage is light enough even for a child to carry.

Why this works

A child being able to carry it illustrates a low degree of weight.

A：は難しかった？ B：泣きたいくらい。

A: Was the exam difficult? B: So difficult I wanted to cry.

Why this works

The emotional reaction communicates the perceived difficulty.

のは、ができないほど。

The mountain summit was so cold that it felt hard to breathe.

Why this works

The physical result emphasizes the speaker's experience of the cold.

Guided checks

立っていられない（　）、足が痛い。

子どもでも読める（　）やさしい本だ。

かさが必要な（　）ではない。

State a personal maximum

～くらい…はない／～ほど…はない

nothing is as…as; there is no…more…than

Formation

N ＋ くらい・ぐらい ＋ N／こと ＋ はない · N ＋ ほど ＋ N／こと ＋ はない

Use this pattern when the speaker selects one thing as the strongest personal example in a category. It often corresponds to “nothing is as…” or “there is no time when I felt more…”.

The statement is usually subjective. It tells us the speaker's judgment, preference, memory, or emotional ranking rather than proving an objective world record.

What to listen for

A category is opened, one member is placed at the top, and the rest are denied with はない.

Compare with 一番

一番 directly marks “number one.” This pattern sounds more expressive because it says no other example reaches the same level.

Common mistake

Keep the negative ending. Without はない, the sentence expresses degree, not the “nothing compares” judgment.

Examples and scenarios

リーさんぐらいなはいない。

No one loves animals as much as Lee does.

Why this works

Lee is presented as the speaker's strongest example of an animal lover.

はをことぐらいことはないと思っています。

I think nothing is as enjoyable as cooking.

Why this works

Cooking is ranked as the speaker's greatest enjoyment.

ああ、ものがある。テストほどいやなものはない。

Oh, there is another kanji test tomorrow. Nothing is as awful as a test.

Why this works

The complaint puts tests at the top of the speaker's disliked things.

２年前にだとわかったときほどになったことはない。

I have never felt as anxious as when I learned two years ago that I was ill.

Why this works

One past moment is selected as the speaker's highest degree of anxiety.

ほどにいいはない。

There is no season as good for walking as spring.

Why this works

Spring is the speaker's top season for walks.

のぐらいはない。

No flavor comforts me as much as my mother's cooking.

Why this works

The comparison expresses an emotional personal maximum.

A：日本で一番好きな町は？ B：ほどはないと思う。

A: Which Japanese city do you like best? B: I don't think any city is as beautiful as Kyoto.

Why this works

The answer turns a preference into an expressive no-equal comparison.

このほどがな仕事はない。

No job requires as much concentration as this one.

Why this works

This job is ranked highest within the speaker's experience.

日ほど日はない。

No day made me happier than the day I passed for the first time.

Why this works

A remembered day is selected as the peak of happiness.

ではほどなものはない。

When traveling, nothing is as important as your health.

Why this works

Health is presented as the speaker's highest travel priority.

Guided checks

春（　）気持ちのいい季節はない。

母の料理ぐらい安心できる味（　）。

試験に合格した日（　）嬉しかった日はない。

Reject an unattractive option

～くらいなら／～ぐらいなら

if it means doing…; rather than do…

Formation

V辞書形 ＋ くらいなら・ぐらいなら、（そのほうがましな選択）

The action before くらいなら is undesirable, unreasonable, or not worth the cost. The second clause states what the speaker would prefer instead—even when that alternative is not ideal.

This is a subjective comparison of bad options. It often carries a strong personal stance, so it can sound blunt when used about another person's choice.

What to listen for

“If that is the price, I would rather choose this other option.”

Compare with ～より

～より neutrally compares two things. ～くらいなら rejects the first option and makes the speaker's dissatisfaction clear.

Common mistake

Do not use the past form before くらいなら. The rejected action is framed with the dictionary form.

Examples and scenarios

でを作るくらいなら、弁当でいい。

If I had to make my own lunch every morning, a convenience-store lunch would be fine.

Why this works

Daily preparation is rejected in favor of the easier alternative.

人といっしょにぐらいなら、このままでいたい。

Rather than live with someone I do not get along with, I would prefer to remain single.

Why this works

The incompatible living arrangement is explicitly rejected.

30分もに行くくらいなら、ほうがいい。

If you would arrive thirty minutes late to the information session, it would be better not to attend.

Why this works

Very late attendance is judged worse than not participating.

ために好きなをくらいなら、今ののままでかまわない。

If losing weight means giving up the cake I love, I do not mind keeping my current figure.

Why this works

The sacrifice is rejected in favor of accepting the current state.

の中を歩くくらいなら、に。

Rather than walk through the rain, let's take a taxi.

Why this works

The speaker rejects the wet walk and chooses the practical alternative.

高いをぐらいなら、駅から少し遠くてもいい。

Rather than pay high rent, I do not mind living a little farther from the station.

Why this works

High rent is weighed against a less convenient but acceptable location.

A：のパソコンを買う？ B：すぐくらいなら、のを買う。

A: Will you buy a used computer? B: If it is going to break immediately, I'll buy a new one.

Why this works

The unreliable cheap option is rejected.

くらいなら、早く朝勉強したほうがいい。

Rather than stay up all night, it is better to sleep early and study in the morning.

Why this works

The harmful study method is rejected for a healthier one.

に全部ぐらいなら、な言葉から復習しよう。

Rather than force yourself to memorize everything, review the important words first.

Why this works

An unrealistic all-at-once strategy is rejected.

いやなをくらいなら、時間をかけてを探したい。

Rather than remain in a job I dislike, I want to take time to search for a new workplace.

Why this works

The speaker prefers a difficult search to the unacceptable status quo.

Guided checks

雨の中を歩く（　）、タクシーに乗ろう。

徹夜するくらいなら、早く（　）ほうがいい。

気の合わない人と住む（　）、一人でいたい。

Recommend the best choice

～に限る

nothing beats; the best choice is; it is best to

Formation

N ＋ に限る · V辞書形／Vない形 ＋ に限る

Use ～に限る for a strong personal recommendation: this is the best thing, method, place, or response in the situation. It often sounds like advice based on experience.

With a negative verb, it recommends avoiding an action: 行かないに限る means that not going is the best policy.

What to listen for

A confident, subjective “this is the best” judgment rather than an objective restriction.

Compare with ～たほうがいい

～たほうがいい advises someone about a particular situation. ～に限る states a broader best-choice judgment and can sound more enthusiastic.

Common mistake

Do not confuse this with the formal notice pattern ～に限り, which means “limited to.” Here the meaning is “is best.”

Examples and scenarios

やっぱりはでに限る。

After all, movies are best watched in a cinema.

Why this works

The speaker enthusiastically recommends the cinema experience.

かぜがいるときは、が多いには行かないに限る。

When colds are going around, the best policy is not to go to crowded places.

Why this works

The negative verb recommends avoidance as the best response.

のを撮るならこのに限ります。すてきな写真が撮れますよ。

If you want to photograph the lake, this is the best place. You can take wonderful pictures here.

Why this works

The place is recommended as the speaker's best viewpoint.

日はに限る。

On a hot day, nothing beats cold barley tea.

Why this works

The speaker recommends a favorite seasonal choice.

ときは早くに限る。

When you are tired, the best thing is to go to bed early.

Why this works

A simple experienced-based remedy is recommended.

A：安くておいしいは？ B：この店のに限るよ。

A: What is a cheap, good lunch? B: You cannot beat this shop's daily set meal.

Why this works

The answer gives a confident personal recommendation.

をなら、毎日に限る。

If you want to improve your Japanese, speaking every day is best.

Why this works

The speaker recommends the most effective habit from experience.

なは確認するに限る。

For important documents, direct confirmation is best.

Why this works

The sentence presents a reliable general policy.

のは家で映画を見るに限る。

On a rainy day off, nothing beats watching a movie at home.

Why this works

The speaker chooses a favorite response to the situation.

の前はにに限る。

Before an exam, getting enough rest is best.

Why this works

The sentence recommends rest as the best exam preparation.

Guided checks

暑い日は冷たいお茶（　）。

疲れたときは早く（　）に限る。

人が多い所には（　）に限る。

Judge degree before choosing a form

Lesson 3 review

Separate degree, personal superlative judgment, rejected alternatives, and best-choice recommendations.

Part 1 · Choose the form

となりの部屋まで聞こえる（　）大きな声だった。

この町（　）静かな所はない。

無理に全部覚える（　）、大切な言葉を復習しよう。

暑い日の飲み物は麦茶（　）。

泣きたい（　）試験が難しかった。

健康（　）大切なものはない。

高い家賃を払う（　）、駅から遠くてもいい。

試験の前は十分に（　）に限る。

Part 2 · Put the sentence in order

Arrange the extreme-degree sentence.

  1. 声だった
  2. 聞こえる
  3. くらい大きな
  4. となりまで

Arrange the no-equal comparison.

  1. ものはない
  2. 健康ほど
  3. 旅行では
  4. 大切な

Arrange the rejected alternative.

  1. タクシーに乗ろう
  2. 歩く
  3. 雨の中を
  4. くらいなら

Arrange the best-choice recommendation.

  1. に限る
  2. 早く
  3. 疲れたときは
  4. 寝る

Part 3 · Complete the mini-passage
