Reject a possibility with strong confidence

1. ～はずがない・～わけがない

Core meaning: Say that something cannot reasonably be true or happen, based on evidence, knowledge, or judgment.

Formation

普通形（ナA・N + な／である）+ はずがない・わけがない

Nuance and use

Both forms are strong. はずがない often follows an expectation based on facts or a logical calculation. わけがない can sound more forceful and personal, as if saying 'there is simply no way.'

Compare

～とは限らない only says exceptions are possible. ～はずがない and ～わけがない reject the possibility much more strongly.

Common mistake

Do not place a negative clause before these forms unless you intend a double negative: 来ないはずがない means 'there is no way he will not come.'

Examples

Book example

ちゃんとしたんだから、彼が来ないはずがない。どうしたのかなあ。

He promised properly, so there is no way he will not come. I wonder what happened.

Why this works

The promise is strong evidence, so the speaker confidently rejects the possibility that he chose not to come.

Book example

あの店が今日休みのはずはありません。電話でしたんですから。

There is no way that shop is closed today. I confirmed it by phone.

Why this works

The telephone confirmation provides objective grounds for the speaker's strong conclusion.

Book example

なのだからはずがない。がんばらなくては……。

It is a national examination, so there is no way it will be easy. I have to work hard...

Why this works

The nature of a national examination supports the forceful judgment expressed by はずがない.

Book example

こんなに大きい家、わたしに買えるわけがないでしょう。

There is no way I could buy such a large house.

Why this works

わけがない rejects the possibility from the speaker's practical circumstances and judgment.

Book example

に勝つためにしているのだ。がきびしくないわけがない。

We are practicing to win the match. There is no way the practice would not be demanding.

Why this works

The purpose of winning makes strict practice an unavoidable, strongly expected fact.

田中さんはしているから、でに負けるはずがない。

Tanaka practices every day, so there is no way he will lose the match easily.

Why this works

The daily practice supplies the evidence behind the confident expectation.

このは先生でも解けなかった。わたしにわけがない。

Even the teacher could not solve this problem. There is no way I can solve it.

Why this works

The comparison with the teacher supports the speaker's forceful personal judgment.

彼はも日本に住んでいる。日本語が話せないはずがない。

He has lived in Japan for ten years. There is no way he cannot speak Japanese.

Why this works

The long residence makes inability contrary to the speaker's expectation.

そんな高い時計を学生のわたしが買えるわけがない。

There is no way a student like me can buy such an expensive watch.

Why this works

わけがない rejects the purchase as impossible under the speaker's financial circumstances.

の直前なのだから、遊んでいるはずがない。きっと図書館にいる。

It is right before the exam, so there is no way she is out having fun. She must be at the library.

Why this works

The timing provides grounds for rejecting one possibility and inferring another.

Guided checks

電話で確認したから、店が休みの（　）。

プロでも難しいのに、初心者にできる（　）。

約束したから、彼が来ない（　）。

Recap: Use these forms when the speaker has strong grounds for ruling a possibility out.

Show that a general rule has exceptions

2. ～とは限らない

Core meaning: Say that something is not always true and that another possibility remains.

Formation

普通形（ナA・N + だ）+ とは限らない

Nuance and use

This form weakens an overconfident rule or assumption. It often appears with words such as いつも, みんな, 必ず, だれでも, or 絶対, because those broad claims invite exceptions.

Compare

～わけがない says something is impossible. ～とは限らない does not choose one outcome; it only warns that the expected outcome is not guaranteed.

Common mistake

A negative clause before the form reverses the surface meaning: 失敗しないとは限らない means failure is possible.

Examples

Book example

このは古くからわれているが、日本人がみんな知っているとは限らない。

Although this song has been sung for a long time, it does not mean every Japanese person knows it.

Why this works

とは限らない limits the generalization that a famous old song must be known by everyone.

Book example

が高いものが必ずがいいとは限らない。

Expensive things are not necessarily always good quality.

Why this works

The pattern directly rejects the universal link between high price and high quality.

Book example

にけがをしないとは限りません。に入っておいたほうがいいですよ。

You might get injured while traveling. It is better to have insurance.

Why this works

The negative clause before とは限らない means the safe outcome is not guaranteed.

Book example

に書いてあることがいつものこと（だ）とは限らない。

What is written in a newspaper is not always true.

Why this works

The form warns that a commonly trusted source can still have exceptions.

有名な店がいつもおいしいとは限らない。

A famous restaurant is not necessarily always delicious.

Why this works

The form challenges the automatic link between fame and quality.

日本に長く住めば日本語が上手になるとは限らない。

Living in Japan a long time does not necessarily make someone good at Japanese.

Why this works

The condition may help, but the result is not guaranteed in every case.

若い人がみんな新しい技術に詳しいとは限りません。

Not all young people are necessarily knowledgeable about new technology.

Why this works

みんな creates a broad generalization that the pattern limits.

努力すれば必ず成功するとは限らないが、努力は必要だ。

Effort does not always guarantee success, but effort is necessary.

Why this works

The form allows exceptions without dismissing the value of effort.

晴れていても暖かいとは限らない。

Even if it is sunny, it is not necessarily warm.

Why this works

The visible weather does not guarantee the expected temperature.

便利な機能が多いほど使いやすいとは限らない。

Having more convenient features does not necessarily make something easier to use.

Why this works

The form questions a common assumption about quantity and usability.

Guided checks

高い物が必ず長く使える（　）。

晴れていても、暖かい（　）。

旅行中に事故が起きない（　）。

Recap: Use ～とは限らない to challenge a generalization without claiming the opposite is certain.

Deny an inference without denying everything

3. ～わけではない・～というわけではない・～のではない

Core meaning: Correct an assumption or deny only part of a statement rather than rejecting the whole situation.

Formation

普通形（ナA・N + な／である）+ わけではない／普通形 + というわけではない／普通形（ナA・N + な）+ のではない

Nuance and use

The listener may reasonably infer one explanation from the situation. These forms say that inference is not the full truth, and the speaker often supplies a narrower or different explanation afterward.

Compare

～わけがない rejects possibility. ～わけではない rejects an interpretation. The single particle で changes the meaning completely.

Common mistake

Do not translate every occurrence as a total 'not.' Look for the specific assumption being corrected and preserve any fact the speaker still accepts.

Examples

Book example

長い間本をお借りしたままでしたが、忘れていたわけではありません。

I kept your book for a long time, but it is not that I had forgotten it.

Why this works

わけではありません denies the likely explanation without denying that the book was kept too long.

Book example

いつでも電話に出られるわけではありません。はメールでお願いします。

It is not that I can answer the phone at any time. Please contact me by email.

Why this works

The pattern partially limits availability rather than saying the speaker can never answer.

Book example

この仕事が好き（だ）というわけではないが、彼といっしょに仕事ができて楽しい。

It is not that I like this job, but I enjoy being able to work with him.

Why this works

というわけではない rejects the broad inference while preserving the more limited positive point.

Book example

するのではありません。をんです。

It is not that I am being transferred. I am leaving the company.

Why this works

のではありません directly corrects an assumed explanation and supplies the actual one.

Book example

A「いいね。高かったでしょう。」B「これは買ったんじゃないの。自分で作ったの。」

A: Nice hat. It must have been expensive. B: I did not buy this. I made it myself.

Why this works

じゃないの conversationally denies only the assumption that the hat was purchased.

甘い物が嫌いなわけではないが、健康のために控えている。

It is not that I dislike sweets; I am cutting back for my health.

Why this works

The speaker denies dislike as the explanation while confirming the reduced consumption.

忙しいからしなかったというわけではありません。メールを失くしたんです。

It is not that I failed to contact you because I was busy. I lost the email.

Why this works

というわけではない rejects one complete explanation and replaces it with the real reason.

日本料理なら何でも好きなわけではない。

It is not that I like every kind of Japanese food.

Why this works

The pattern partially limits a possible overgeneralization about the speaker's taste.

会議に反対しているのではない。もう少し資料が必要だと思うのだ。

It is not that I oppose the meeting. I think we need a little more information.

Why this works

のではない corrects the inferred attitude and states a more precise concern.

だけでこの商品を選んだわけではありません。

It is not that I chose this product only because of the price.

Why this works

The sentence denies price as the sole reason while leaving open that price mattered somewhat.

Guided checks

嫌いな（　）が、今日は食べたくない。

転勤する（　）。会社を辞めるんです。

安いから選んだ（　）。使いやすさも大切だった。

Recap: Use these forms to say 'it is not that...' and narrow or correct an apparent conclusion.

Give a cautious or reluctant yes

4. ～ないことはない

Core meaning: Say that something is possible or somewhat true, while implying a reservation.

Formation

Vない形／イAくない／ナA・Nではない + ことはない

Nuance and use

The double negative does not sound as positive as a direct statement. It often means 'I could,' 'it is not impossible,' or 'I cannot say it is not,' and a limitation normally follows.

Compare

A direct positive sounds confident. ～ないことはない sounds hesitant, qualified, or reluctant and prepares the listener for a drawback.

Common mistake

Do not use it when you intend an enthusiastic yes. Without an understood reservation, the double negative can sound evasive.

Examples

Book example

ここからまで歩けないことはありませんが、かなりがかかりますよ。

It is not impossible to walk from here to the station, but it will take quite a while.

Why this works

The double negative gives a reluctant yes, immediately followed by a practical limitation.

Book example

この店のカレーもおいしくないことはないが、わたしはもっとのが好きだ。

This shop's curry is not bad, but I prefer something spicier.

Why this works

The speaker weakly acknowledges that the curry is good before stating a preference.

Book example

のがでないことはないのですが、今は終わってほっとしています。

I cannot say I am not worried about the exam result, but now that it is over I feel relieved.

Why this works

The double negative admits some concern without making it the speaker's strongest feeling.

今日中に終わらせられないことはないが、かなり急ぐ必要がある。

It is not impossible to finish today, but we will need to hurry considerably.

Why this works

The speaker accepts feasibility while immediately naming the demanding condition.

この色も悪くないことはないけれど、青のほうが似合う。

This color is not bad, but blue suits you better.

Why this works

The double negative offers mild approval rather than a strong compliment.

一人で行けないことはないが、少し不安だ。

I could go alone, but I feel a little uneasy.

Why this works

The possibility is admitted, and the following concern explains the hesitation.

彼の説明がわからないことはなかったが、例がもっと欲しかった。

It was not that I could not understand his explanation, but I wanted more examples.

Why this works

The speaker weakly confirms understanding while noting what was missing.

その仕事を引き受けられないことはありません。条件をさせてください。

It is not impossible for me to accept the job. Please let me confirm the conditions.

Why this works

The polite double negative leaves acceptance open but not final.

この料理は食べられないことはないが、辛すぎる。

I can eat this dish, but it is too spicy.

Why this works

The speaker concedes ability while clearly limiting enjoyment.

電車でも行けないことはないけれど、車のほうが早い。

We could go by train, but going by car is faster.

Why this works

The train remains possible, though it is not the preferred choice.

Guided checks

歩いて行けない（　）が、時間がかかる。

この料理もおいしくない（　）が、前の店のほうが好きだ。

手伝えない（　）。ただ、少し遅くなる。

Recap: Use ～ないことはない for a qualified yes that still carries doubt or limitation.

Concede one fact before adding a limitation

5. ～ことは～が、…

Core meaning: Acknowledge that something is true, then contrast it with a reservation, limit, or less important fact.

Formation

V・イA普通形 + ことは + V・イA普通形 + が／ナA・N + なことは + ナA・N + だが

Nuance and use

The key word is repeated around ことは. The speaker grants the first point instead of arguing about it, then uses が or けれど to show why that fact does not settle the matter.

Compare

～ないことはない gives a weak positive through double negation. ～ことは～が directly concedes the repeated positive or descriptive predicate.

Common mistake

Repeat the same predicate on both sides of ことは. Changing it produces a different structure and loses the deliberate concession.

Examples

Book example

彼からの手紙は読んだことは読んだんですが、がよくわかりませんでした。

I did read his letter, but I did not understand the meaning well.

Why this works

Repeating 読む around ことは concedes that the action happened, then が introduces the limitation.

Book example

わたしは泳げることは泳げますが、長いはだめなんです。

I can swim, but I cannot manage long distances.

Why this works

The repeated potential verb grants basic ability while limiting its extent.

Book example

この本は高いことは高いが、が多くて楽しめそうだ。

This book is expensive, but it has many photographs and looks enjoyable.

Why this works

The adjective is repeated to acknowledge the price before giving a balancing positive point.

Book example

をのはなことはだが、が楽しみでさを。

Raising children is certainly difficult, but their growth is enjoyable and makes you forget the hardship.

Why this works

The na-adjective phrase is repeated to concede the difficulty before the contrasting reward.

この部屋は広いことは広いが、から遠い。

This room is certainly spacious, but it is far from the station.

Why this works

広い is repeated to concede the advantage before the location drawback.

彼の説明はわかることはわかるけれど、まだ納得できない。

I understand his explanation, but I still cannot accept it.

Why this works

The repeated verb grants comprehension while distinguishing it from agreement.

その映画は面白いことは面白いが、少し長すぎる。

The movie is interesting, but it is a little too long.

Why this works

The positive evaluation is conceded before the limitation on length.

まで近いことは近いですが、道が暗いです。

It is true that the station is close, but the road is dark.

Why this works

近い is repeated politely to acknowledge distance while emphasizing safety concerns.

料理はできることはできるが、得意ではない。

I can cook, but I am not good at it.

Why this works

The repeated potential verb grants basic ability without claiming skill.

この案は便利なことは便利だが、費用が高い。

This plan is certainly convenient, but its cost is high.

Why this works

The na-adjective is repeated with なことは便利だ to concede convenience before cost.

Guided checks

泳げる（　）が、長い距離は無理だ。

この本は高い（　）が、内容は役に立つ。

便利な（　）が、費用がかかる。

Recap: Use ～ことは～が to say 'it is true that..., but...' while keeping the limitation central.
