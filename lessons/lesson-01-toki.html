Before a situation changes

1. ～うちに

Core meaning: while; during; before the present state changes

Formation

Nの／Vる／Vている／Vない／いA／なAな ＋ うちに

Nuance and use

Use ～うちに when the current condition creates a temporary opportunity. The action after the pattern should happen before that condition ends or changes.

A second use describes a change that develops naturally during a continuing activity. In that use, the change is usually not a deliberate goal.

A temporary state, a useful opportunity, or a gradual change during an ongoing experience.

Compare

～間に simply places an event inside a time period. ～うちに adds the feeling that the opportunity may disappear or that a change develops during the period.

Common mistake

Do not use this pattern for a permanent state with no meaningful boundary. The listener should be able to imagine the condition changing.

Examples

Book example

にいるうちに一度にみたい。

I would like to try climbing Mount Fuji once while I am in Japan.

Why this works

The stay in Japan is temporary, so it creates a limited opportunity for the climb.

Book example

はい、アイスクリーム。うちに食べてくださいね。

Here is your ice cream. Please eat it quickly before it melts.

Why this works

The negative form 溶けないうちに marks the short opportunity before melting begins.

Book example

うちにのをしてしまおう。

Let’s finish cleaning the garden while it is still light.

Why this works

The deliberate cleaning should be completed before the daylight disappears.

Book example

をいるうちにしまった。

I fell asleep while listening to music.

Why this works

Falling asleep is an unintentional change that occurs during the continuing listening.

Book example

少しでも、をうちにようになりますよ。

Even with a slightly difficult piece, you will become able to play it as you continue practicing.

Why this works

Ability develops gradually and unintentionally over the continuing practice period.

Book example

うちにはなっていた。

Before I realized it, it had become dark outside.

Why this works

The outside condition changes without the speaker consciously causing or noticing it.

A：この、いい？
B：ならないうちにほうがいいよ。

A: Can I eat this bread tomorrow?
B: You should eat it before it gets hard.

Why this works

The bread’s soft condition will change, so eating it earlier is recommended.

A：に？
B：まだ。がうちにみる。

A: Did you ask the manager?
B: Not yet. I’ll try asking before the meeting ends.

Why this works

The manager is available only until the meeting finishes.

ののはそうなので、うちに。

I heard the old hotel near the sea will close next month, so I want to stay there once before it is gone.

Why this works

The hotel’s continued existence is a disappearing opportunity.

でいるうちに、のをようになった。

While working overseas, I gradually became able to express my opinions clearly.

Why this works

This is the developmental use: the ability changed naturally during an ongoing experience.

Guided checks

電車が混まない（　）、出かけましょう。

元気な（　）、いろいろな町を歩きたい。

留学している（　）、一度富士山に登った。

Recap: Use ～うちに when you need this relationship: while; during; before the present state changes .

Duration or one event inside it

2. ～間／～間に

Core meaning: during the whole period / at some point within the period

Formation

Nの／Vている／Vない ＋ 間（に）

Nuance and use

Use ～間 when the action or state after it continues through the same period. Use ～間に when a bounded event happens once or a limited number of times inside that period.

If the second action can naturally continue from the start to the end, choose 間. If it has a clear completion point, choose 間に.

Compare

～間に locates the event in time. ～うちに emphasizes a changing opportunity or a gradual development.

Common mistake

Do not add に merely because the English translation says “during.” Decide whether the second event continues or finishes.

Examples

Book example

がをしている間、たちはテレビを見ていた。

While their mother was taking a nap, the children were watching television.

Why this works

Both the nap and the television watching continue through the same period, so 間 is used.

Book example

わたしがでの間、うちののをお願いできないでしょうか。

Could I ask you to take care of my dog while I am away traveling?

Why this works

The absence and the requested care both extend through the trip, so 間 fits.

Book example

がをしている間に、たちは。

While their mother was napping, the children went out to play.

Why this works

Going out is one completed event inside the longer nap, so 間に is used.

Book example

わたしがでの間に、にがたくさんしまった。

While I was away traveling, a lot of grass grew in the garden.

Why this works

The growth is a change that occurs within the longer absence, so 間に fits.

のは、のをください。

Please keep your mobile phone turned off during the exam.

Why this works

The phone must remain off throughout the exam period.

がの、が。

A large package arrived while the family was away.

Why this works

The delivery is a completed event within the absence.

A：をいる、でもらえますか。
B：はい、です。

A: Could you wait here while I copy the documents?
B: Yes, that’s fine.

Why this works

The waiting continues for the whole copying period.

A：の、はどうするの？
B：によ。

A: What will you do with the cat during your business trip?
B: I’ll leave it with my younger sister.

Why this works

The cat will stay with the sister throughout the trip.

がのをいる、はのを。

While the clerk looked for another size, I checked the color of the shoes once more.

Why this works

The check is completed once inside the clerk’s search period.

がいる、のはほどだった。

While the baby was napping, the house was surprisingly quiet.

Why this works

The quiet state continues throughout the nap.

Guided checks

先生が説明している（　）、学生は静かに聞いていた。

昼休みの（　）、銀行へ行ってきた。

雨が降っている（　）、ずっと家にいた。

Recap: Use ～間／～間に when you need this relationship: during the whole period / at some point within the period .

A required first step

3. ～てからでないと／～てからでなければ

Core meaning: not until; unless the first action is completed

Formation

Vて ＋ からでないと／からでなければ ＋ negative or difficult result

Nuance and use

Use this pattern when action A is a necessary condition for action B. The speaker says that B cannot, must not, or is difficult to do before A has been completed.

～てからでないと is common in conversation. ～てからでなければ is slightly more formal, but the core meaning is the same.

A rule, prerequisite, practical limitation, or judgment that the next step is impossible too early.

Compare

朝ご飯を食べてから出かけた only reports the order “I ate, then left.” 食べてからでないと出かけられない says eating is required before leaving is possible.

Common mistake

The result normally contains a negative or limiting idea such as ～ない、～できない、～てはいけない、難しい. Do not follow it with an ordinary positive result.

Examples

Book example

「いかがですか。こちらのはですよ。」客「うーん。物なので、とからでないと買うかどうかね。」

Clerk: “How about it? This painting is wonderful.” Customer: “Hmm. It is expensive, so I cannot decide whether to buy it until I consult my family.”

Why this works

Consulting the family is the required step before the customer can make the decision.

Book example

を取ってからでなければをはいけない。

You must not drive a car until you have obtained a driver’s license.

Why this works

The license is a legal prerequisite, and the result expresses prohibition.

Book example

がからでなければはだ。

Strenuous exercise is impossible until the illness has healed.

Why this works

Recovery must occur first, and 無理 supplies the required limiting result.

このはをしてからでないと、いけません。

You must not take this medicine until after eating.

Why this works

Eating is a health-related condition that must come first.

のをからでなければ、かどうか。

I cannot decide whether to buy it until I see the actual product.

Why this works

Seeing the item is the evidence needed before making a decision.

をからでないと、このは。

You cannot use this service until you set a password.

Why this works

Password setup is a system prerequisite for access.

A：もうか。
B：いいえ、にをしてからでないと、は。

A: Can I apply now?
B: No. The procedure will not be completed until you pay first.

Why this works

Payment is the required condition for completing the application.

A：もうを？
B：をからでないと、よ。

A: Are you starting to cook already?
B: I cannot start until I buy all the ingredients.

Why this works

Having every ingredient is a practical prerequisite.

をからでなければ、をはいけない。

You must not operate the machine until you confirm that it is safe.

Why this works

The first action is an explicit safety requirement.

のをからでないと、なをのは。

It is difficult to understand the complicated procedure correctly until you hear the staff member’s explanation.

Why this works

The negative force appears as 難しい rather than a direct ～ない ending.

Guided checks

会員登録をして（　）、この動画は見られません。

料金を払って（　）、中には入れない。

朝ご飯を食べて（　）、学校へ行った。

Recap: Use ～てからでないと／～てからでなければ when you need this relationship: not until; unless the first action is completed .

Zoom in on one stage

4. ～ところだ

Core meaning: be just about to; be right in the middle of; have just finished

Formation

Vるところだ · Vているところだ · Vたところだ

Nuance and use

This pattern places the listener at a precise stage of an action. The verb form acts like a timeline: dictionary form is immediately before, ている is during, and past form is immediately after.

Particles after ところ come from the following clause. For example, ～ところに／へ marks an event arriving at that moment, and ～ところを marks the scene or person that someone sees, catches, or interrupts.

食べるところ → not started; 食べているところ → in progress; 食べたところ → just completed.

Compare

～たところ focuses on the exact moment immediately after completion. ～たばかり means “recently” from the speaker’s viewpoint and can cover a longer period.

Common mistake

Choose the verb form from the action’s real stage, not from the tense of the whole sentence. Also avoid this pattern with states that have no clear beginning, middle, or end.

Examples

Book example

ロケットは間もなくところです。のです。

The rocket is about to lift off. It is a tense moment.

Why this works

The dictionary form 飛び立つ places the rocket immediately before liftoff.

Book example

、の人のを見ているところをに。

During the exam, the teacher warned me when I was looking at the person next to me’s answer.

Why this works

ところを marks the exact ongoing scene that the teacher noticed and interrupted.

Book example

テレビドラマがところでが。

Just as the television drama I had been looking forward to began, the phone rang.

Why this works

Vたところで fixes the interruption at the moment immediately after the drama started.

Book example

ケーキがところへたちがきた。

The children came home just as the cake was finished.

Why this works

ところへ presents their arrival as entering the scene immediately after completion.

についているところへ、がきた。

The teacher came in while we were discussing the answer.

Why this works

～ところへ presents the teacher’s arrival as entering the ongoing scene.

をいるところに、が。

The manager came just as I was copying the documents.

Why this works

～ところに marks another event arriving during the action.

A：はもういる？
B：うん、ところ。

A: Have you started writing the report?
B: Yes, I have just started now.

Why this works

The past form focuses on the just-completed start of the writing process.

A：はいますか。
B：すみません。たったところです。

A: Is Ms. Yamada here?
B: Sorry. She just went home.

Why this works

The departure happened moments before the question.

がところで、が、が。

Just as the train was about to depart, the emergency bell rang and the service was stopped.

Why this works

The train is frozen at the stage immediately before departure when the interruption occurs.

がところだったので、へことにした。

The rain had just stopped, so I decided to go outside.

Why this works

The decision is made at the moment immediately after the rain ends.

Guided checks

今、昼ご飯を食べて（　）ところです。

たった今、駅に着い（　）ところです。

これから出かけ（　）ところなので、あとで話しましょう。

Recap: Use ～ところだ when you need this relationship: be just about to; be right in the middle of; have just finished .
