1. ～うちに

Formation

Nの／Vる／Vている／Vない／いA／なAな ＋ うちに

Book explanation

Use ～うちに when the current condition creates a temporary opportunity. The action after the pattern should happen before that condition ends or changes. A second use describes a change that develops naturally during a continuing activity. In that use, the change is usually not a deliberate goal. A temporary state, a useful opportunity, or a gradual change during an ongoing experience.

English study note

Core meaning: while; during; before the present state changes Compare ～間に simply places an event inside a time period. ～うちに adds the feeling that the opportunity may disappear or that a change develops during the period. Common mistake Do not use this pattern for a permanent state with no meaningful boundary. The listener should be able to imagine the condition changing.

Book examples

Book example

にいるうちににみたい。

I would like to try climbing Mount Fuji once while I am in Japan.

Why this works

The stay in Japan is temporary, so it creates a limited opportunity for the climb.

Book example

はい、アイスクリーム。うちにくださいね。

Here is your ice cream. Please eat it quickly before it melts.

Why this works

The negative form 溶けないうちに marks the short opportunity before melting begins.

Book example

うちにのをしてしまおう。

Let’s finish cleaning the garden while it is still light.

Why this works

The deliberate cleaning should be completed before the daylight disappears.

Book example

をいるうちにしまった。

I fell asleep while listening to music.

Why this works

Falling asleep is an unintentional change that occurs during the continuing listening.

Book example

でも、をうちにようになりますよ。

Even with a slightly difficult piece, you will become able to play it as you continue practicing.

Why this works

Ability develops gradually and unintentionally over the continuing practice period.

Book example

がつかないうちにはなっていた。

Before I realized it, it had become dark outside.

Why this works

The outside condition changes without the speaker consciously causing or noticing it.

2. ～間／～間に

Formation

Nの／Vている／Vない ＋ （に）

Book explanation

Use ～間 when the action or state after it continues through the same period. Use ～間に when a bounded event happens once or a limited number of times inside that period. If the second action can naturally continue from the start to the end, choose 間. If it has a clear completion point, choose 間に.

English study note

Core meaning: during the whole period / at some point within the period Compare ～間に locates the event in time. ～うちに emphasizes a changing opportunity or a gradual development. Common mistake Do not add に merely because the English translation says “during.” Decide whether the second event continues or finishes.

Book examples

Book example

がをしている、たちはテレビをいた。

While their mother was taking a nap, the children were watching television.

Why this works

Both the nap and the television watching continue through the same period, so 間 is used.

Book example

わたしがでの、うちののをできないでしょうか。

Could I ask you to take care of my dog while I am away traveling?

Why this works

The absence and the requested care both extend through the trip, so 間 fits.

Book example

がをしている、たちはに。

While their mother was napping, the children went out to play.

Why this works

Going out is one completed event inside the longer nap, so 間に is used.

Book example

わたしがでの、にがたくさんしまった。

While I was away traveling, a lot of grass grew in the garden.

Why this works

The growth is a change that occurs within the longer absence, so 間に fits.

3. ～てからでないと／～てからでなければ

Formation

Vて ＋ からでないと／からでなければ ＋ negative or difficult result

Book explanation

Use this pattern when action A is a necessary condition for action B. The speaker says that B cannot, must not, or is difficult to do before A has been completed. ～てからでないと is common in conversation. ～てからでなければ is slightly more formal, but the core meaning is the same. A rule, prerequisite, practical limitation, or judgment that the next step is impossible too early.

English study note

Core meaning: not until; unless the first action is completed Compare 朝ご飯を食べてから出かけた only reports the order “I ate, then left.” 食べてからでないと出かけられない says eating is required before leaving is possible. Common mistake The result normally contains a negative or limiting idea such as ～ない、～できない、～てはいけない、難しい. Do not follow it with an ordinary positive result.

Book examples

Book example

「いかがですか。こちらのはすばらしいですよ。」「うーん。なので、とからでないとかどうかられませんね。」

Clerk: “How about it? This painting is wonderful.” Customer: “Hmm. It is expensive, so I cannot decide whether to buy it until I consult my family.”

Why this works

Consulting the family is the required step before the customer can make the decision.

Book example

をからでなければをはいけない。

You must not drive a car until you have obtained a driver’s license.

Why this works

The license is a legal prerequisite, and the result expresses prohibition.

Book example

がからでなければはだ。

Strenuous exercise is impossible until the illness has healed.

Why this works

Recovery must occur first, and 無理 supplies the required limiting result.

4. ～ところだ

Formation

Vるところだ · Vているところだ · Vたところだ

Book explanation

This pattern places the listener at a precise stage of an action. The verb form acts like a timeline: dictionary form is immediately before, ている is during, and past form is immediately after. Particles after ところ come from the following clause. For example, ～ところに／へ marks an event arriving at that moment, and ～ところを marks the scene or person that someone sees, catches, or interrupts. 食べるところ → not started; 食べているところ → in progress; 食べたところ → just completed.

English study note

Core meaning: be just about to; be right in the middle of; have just finished Compare ～たところ focuses on the exact moment immediately after completion. ～たばかり means “recently” from the speaker’s viewpoint and can cover a longer period. Common mistake Choose the verb form from the action’s real stage, not from the tense of the whole sentence. Also avoid this pattern with states that have no clear beginning, middle, or end.

Book examples

Book example

ロケットはところです。のです。

The rocket is about to lift off. It is a tense moment.

Why this works

The dictionary form 飛び立つ places the rocket immediately before liftoff.

Book example

、となりののをいるところをにされた。

During the exam, the teacher warned me when I was looking at the person next to me’s answer.

Why this works

ところを marks the exact ongoing scene that the teacher noticed and interrupted.

Book example

にしていたテレビドラマがところでが。

Just as the television drama I had been looking forward to began, the phone rang.

Why this works

Vたところで fixes the interruption at the moment immediately after the drama started.

Book example

ケーキがところへたちがきた。

The children came home just as the cake was finished.

Why this works

ところへ presents their arrival as entering the scene immediately after completion.
